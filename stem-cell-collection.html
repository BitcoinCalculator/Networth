Optia CMNC — Quality Optimization Tips
Universal best-practice tips for any HPC-A run. Product-specific overrides (e.g., Bluebird "collect dark") appear in the company info card above when selected.
Collection Preference (color tool)
Where it lives: the Spectra Optia "Collection Preference Tool" — a printed color strip showing dark red/brown → orange → light peach. Lower numbers = lighter (more plasma in product), higher numbers = darker (more cells in product).
When to adjust: only after the message bar reads "Collecting MNC". Change in increments of 10, wait ≥ 2 minutes between adjustments, then assess the collect-line color.
Standard / CAR-T (most products): aim for the lighter end (less RBC contamination, cleaner T-cell or MNC product).
Bluebird gene therapy (SCD/TDT): aim for the darker end — the manufacturer requires high CD34 yield per session and tolerates higher product Hct.
Therapeutic WBC depletion: moderate to slightly darker is reasonable to maximize WBC removal — verify against your institutional protocol.
Inlet Flow & Alarm Discipline
Steady > fast. "Slower with NO alarms is more efficient than faster WITH alarms." Each alarm pause interrupts MNC layer formation and degrades CE2.
Aim for stability. Find the maximum inlet flow that runs continuously without high-pressure or interface alarms. Hold there.
Access matters. Central catheter ≥ 10 Fr for adults; for peripheral, hot pack + tourniquet, large-bore (16–17 g) with countertraction.
Anticoagulation Balance & AIR (the number to watch)
What you set: inlet flow (mL/min) and ACD-A ratio (whole blood : AC). AIR is not something you set — it's a calculated value displayed on the Optia screen.
What AIR is: AC Infusion Rate to the patient, in mL/min/L TBV. It tells you the citrate dose-rate. Most runs sit comfortably below 1.0; the closer to 1.2 (machine ceiling), the more likely the patient feels citrate symptoms.
Why the cap matters: the Optia will not let inlet flow push AIR above 1.2. So if you tighten AC ratio (e.g., 12:1 → 8:1), the machine effectively caps your inlet flow at a lower number — total whole-blood processed in the same time goes down.
Math: Max inlet (mL/min) = 1.2 × AC ratio × TBV(L). Example for a 5 L TBV patient: 12:1 → 72 mL/min cap · 8:1 → 48 mL/min · 6:1 → 36 mL/min.
Standard CMNC start: 12:1. Drop to 10:1 or 8:1 if clumping. For Bluebird SCD/TDT: start 8:1, drop to 6:1 if needed — but expect throughput to drop with each step tighter.
Watch the line: visible clumps in the collect line → tighten AC immediately; expect AIR to climb and inlet to drop. Watch the patient for citrate symptoms when AIR creeps over 1.0.
To pull AIR down without changing inlet → loosen AC ratio. To recover throughput when AIR is at 1.2 → loosen AC ratio so the cap permits higher inlet.
Citrate / Electrolyte Management
Calcium gluconate infusion smooths perioral tingling, cramping, and lightheadedness.
Don't over-correct Ca²⁺. Excess ionized calcium can contribute to clumping, especially with rich AC ratios.
Mg²⁺ and K⁺ replacement: consider when symptoms persist despite calcium and magnesium is low — citrate also chelates Mg.
Pre-meal: a meal + oral calcium ~30 min before start blunts citrate symptoms.
Buffy Coat & Centrifuge Care
Watch the buffy coat layer on the channel as the run progresses — accumulation can predict end-of-run performance and signal need for collect-pref adjustment.
Centrifuge cleanliness: a dirty / poorly maintained centrifuge degrades CE2. Wipe down between runs; report excess vibration to biomed.
Pre-CD34 / Plerixafor Decision Support
≥ 20 /µL: proceed; standard 2× TBV expected to reach target.
10–19 /µL: proceed but consider plerixafor (0.24 mg/kg SQ ~11 h pre next collection) and consider LVL (≥ 3× TBV).
5–9 /µL: add plerixafor or remobilize.
< 5 /µL: remobilization recommended — collection unlikely to be productive.
